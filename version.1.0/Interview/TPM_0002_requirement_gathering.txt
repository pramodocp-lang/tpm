====================================================
TPM INTERVIEW Q&A - REQUIREMENT GATHERING
====================================================

Q1. What is Requirement Gathering?

Answer:
Requirement Gathering is the first phase of SDLC where business needs are collected, analyzed, and documented. The goal is to clearly understand what needs to be built before moving into design and development.

----------------------------------------------------

Q2. What is your role as a Technical Project Manager during Requirement Gathering?

Answer:
As a TPM, I work with stakeholders to understand business objectives, clarify requirements, identify dependencies and risks, facilitate discussions between business and engineering teams, and ensure requirements are documented, prioritized, and ready for implementation.

----------------------------------------------------

Q3. How do you gather requirements from stakeholders?

Answer:
I conduct stakeholder meetings, workshops, and requirement review sessions. I ask clarifying questions, document business needs, identify success criteria, and ensure alignment between business and technical teams.

----------------------------------------------------

Q4. What documents are typically created during Requirement Gathering?

Answer:
- Business Requirement Document (BRD)
- Functional Requirement Document (FRD)
- User Stories
- Acceptance Criteria
- Process Flow Diagrams
- Risk and Dependency Logs

----------------------------------------------------

Q5. What is the difference between BRD and FRD?

Answer:
BRD focuses on business objectives and high-level requirements, while FRD provides detailed functional specifications describing how the system should behave.

----------------------------------------------------

Q6. How do you convert requirements into Jira items?

Answer:
I break down business requirements into Epics, User Stories, Tasks, and Sub-tasks. Each story contains acceptance criteria, priorities, estimates, dependencies, and business value.

----------------------------------------------------

Q7. What is a User Story?

Answer:
A User Story describes a requirement from the user's perspective.

Format:
As a <user>,
I want <feature>,
So that <benefit>.

Example:
As a user, I want to reset my password so that I can regain access to my account.

----------------------------------------------------

Q8. What are Acceptance Criteria?

Answer:
Acceptance Criteria define the conditions that must be met for a user story to be considered complete and accepted by stakeholders.

----------------------------------------------------

Q9. How do you handle ambiguous requirements?

Answer:
I schedule clarification meetings, ask probing questions, document assumptions, create process flows or mockups, and obtain stakeholder sign-off before development begins.

----------------------------------------------------

Q10. What is Scope Creep?

Answer:
Scope creep occurs when new requirements are added without proper evaluation, approval, or adjustment to timeline and resources.

Mitigation:
- Change Control Process
- Impact Analysis
- Stakeholder Approval
- Requirement Traceability

----------------------------------------------------

Q11. How do you prioritize requirements?

Answer:
I work with stakeholders using prioritization techniques such as:
- MoSCoW (Must, Should, Could, Won't)
- Business Value
- Risk Impact
- Technical Dependency
- Customer Impact

----------------------------------------------------

Q12. What are Functional Requirements?

Answer:
Functional requirements describe what the system should do.

Examples:
- User Login
- Password Reset
- Search Functionality
- Report Generation

----------------------------------------------------

Q13. What are Non-Functional Requirements?

Answer:
Non-functional requirements define system quality attributes.

Examples:
- Performance
- Security
- Scalability
- Availability
- Reliability

----------------------------------------------------

Q14. How do you identify dependencies?

Answer:
I collaborate with engineering, infrastructure, security, and external teams to identify systems, services, APIs, approvals, and integrations required for successful delivery.

----------------------------------------------------

Q15. How do you manage risks during Requirement Gathering?

Answer:
I identify potential risks early, assess impact and probability, create mitigation plans, assign owners, and track risks throughout the project lifecycle.

----------------------------------------------------

Q16. How do you ensure requirements are ready for development?

Answer:
I ensure:
- Requirements are clear and complete.
- Acceptance criteria are defined.
- Dependencies are identified.
- Estimates are completed.
- Stakeholder approval is obtained.

----------------------------------------------------

Q17. What challenges have you faced during Requirement Gathering?

Answer:
Common challenges include:
- Unclear requirements
- Conflicting stakeholder expectations
- Frequent scope changes
- Missing dependencies
- Tight timelines

I address these through structured discussions, documentation, prioritization, and regular stakeholder communication.

----------------------------------------------------

Q18. How do you run a Requirement Gathering Meeting?

Answer:
1. Define meeting objectives.
2. Identify stakeholders.
3. Discuss business goals.
4. Capture requirements.
5. Clarify assumptions.
6. Identify risks and dependencies.
7. Document decisions.
8. Share meeting notes and action items.

----------------------------------------------------

Q19. How do you measure success in Requirement Gathering?

Answer:
Success is measured by:
- Clear and approved requirements
- Minimal requirement changes
- Reduced rework
- High stakeholder alignment
- Smooth transition to development

----------------------------------------------------

Q20. Tell me about your Requirement Gathering process as a TPM.

Answer:
I start by understanding business objectives and stakeholder expectations. I conduct requirement workshops, document functional and non-functional requirements, identify dependencies and risks, convert requirements into Epics and User Stories in Jira, define acceptance criteria, facilitate estimation discussions, and obtain stakeholder approval before execution begins.
